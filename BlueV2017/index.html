    # “Hear What You Want”: Sonic Politics, Blackness, and Racism-Canceling Headphones

    Author: !Alex Blue V Year: 2017

    # What is the thesis or main argument?

    Uses an advertising campaign for Beats’ noise-cancelling headphones to explore the complex relations between Black subjectivity, consumerism, sound, space, and technology.

    • the commercials frame its headphones as helping Black athletes resist the “haters” around by tuning out, making Black resistance a technologcical commodity for the (white) consumer (neutral and unmarked subjects are always signified as white)
      • racial capitalism
    • commodified anger
      • Is this related to racial performativity?

    Also challenges the idea that Black subjects are inherently resistant, rather than simply existing (see resistance vs existence).

    # Are there any significant side arguments?

    • sound is political
      • noise is a form of othered sound
      • sound is always filtered through aspects of human subjectivity (p. 91)
    • listening as active and hearing as passive
      • uses this differentiation to consider how noise-cancelling headphone technology is constructed to give users more agency over what one hears, although Blue critiques that this control is not as clear-cut—once must also shut of one’s other senses to avoid hearing the world
    • silence as a Black survival technique
      • headphones silence the world, but also the user
    • consumerism and advertising
      • consumerism often relies on white fascination with Black bodies
      • consumers want to feel victorious

    # How does this fit within the literature? Historiography? Is it adding to the arguments already out there? Does it challenge dominant trends in the literature?

    Most sound studies only considers the intersections of Blackness and sound as it relates to music—this article offers something new.
